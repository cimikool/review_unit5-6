Unit 5 · Grammar
The Passive Voice
Used when the action is more important than who does it, or when the agent is unknown.
📐Passive Tense Reference
TenseFormulaExample
Present Simple is / are + past participle Women are elected to parliament every year.
Past Simple was / were + past participle The first female MP was appointed in 1963.
Present Perfect has / have been + past participle Many laws have been changed to protect women.
Future Simple will be + past participle The new law will be signed next month.
🔄 Active → Passive: The Rule
1. The object of the active sentence becomes the subject.
2. The verb changes to be + past participle.
3. The agent (doer) is added with by — or omitted if unknown/unimportant.
✦ Active:
The government appointed the first female minister in 1985.
✦ Passive:
The first female minister was appointed (by the government) in 1985.
⚠️ Common Mistakes
  • ✗ The law was change last year.  →  ✓ was changed
  • ✗ She was elected by in 2022.  →  ✓ was elected in 2022 (no agent needed)
  • ✗ The report is wrote by her.  →  ✓ is written by her
Unit 5 · Communication
Concession & Contrast
Expressing two opposing or contrasting ideas in the same sentence or paragraph.
📋Connectors Reference Table
ConnectorRule / StructureExample
Although
Even though		 + clause (S + V)
Can begin or be in the middle		 Although women have made progress, inequality still exists.
Despite
In spite of		 + noun / noun phrase
+ -ing form		 Despite working hard, she was not promoted.
However
Nevertheless		 Sentence connector
Starts a new sentence, comma after		 Progress has been made. However, challenges remain.
While / Whereas + clause
Shows direct contrast		 While some countries have female leaders, others do not.
On the other hand Used to introduce the opposing side in a discussion Women make excellent leaders. On the other hand, they face more obstacles.
🔑 Key Distinction: Although vs. Despite
  • Although she worked hard, she didn't get the job.  → followed by a full clause (S+V)
  • Despite working hard, she didn't get the job.  → followed by -ing (no subject)
  • Despite her hard work, she didn't get the job.  → followed by a noun phrase
Unit 6 · Grammar
Phrasal Verbs
A verb + particle (preposition or adverb) that together create a new meaning.
📐Types of Phrasal Verbs
Type 1 — Intransitive (no object)
These cannot be separated.
  • The ceremony broke up late at night. (ended)
  • She grew up in a multicultural environment.
Type 2 — Transitive Separable (with object)
Object can go between verb and particle, or after it.
  • They called off the event. / They called the event off.
  • She gave up her old habits to integrate into the new culture.
Type 3 — Transitive Inseparable (three-part)
Object always comes after the particle — cannot be separated.
  • You should put up with cultural differences when abroad.
  • Children look up to their parents as role models.

Making Apologies & Complaining
Key expressions for expressing regret and making/responding to complaints politely.
🙏Making an Apology
FunctionExpressions
Apologising I'm (really/truly/so) sorry for …

Explaining / Giving a reason The reason was that …
It happened because …

Making a promise I promise it won't happen again.
I will make sure this doesn't happen again.
Offering a solution To make up for this, I would like to …
I would be happy to …
Accepting an apology That's all right. / No problem. / Don't worry about it.
I quite understand.

😤Making a Complaint
RegisterExpressions
Polite / Formal I'm afraid there is a problem with …
I must say I was quite surprised / disappointed by …
I wonder if you could explain why …
I find it rather unacceptable that …
Informal I'm not happy about …
I can't stand it when …
It really bothers me that …
Responding to a complaint You're absolutely right, and I'm sorry about that.
I do apologise. I'll look into it immediately.
I understand your concern. Let me explain …
✉️ In a Letter — How to Structure an Apology
1. Acknowledge the problem: I am writing to apologise for …
2. Explain the reason: The reason for this was that …
3. Express regret: I am truly sorry for any trouble this may have caused.
4. Offer a solution or promise: I promise to … / To make up for this …
5. Close politely: I hope you will accept my apology.

📝 Writing Task
Your British pen friend visited your family in Morocco last month. During the visit, you accidentally broke something valuable that belonged to them. Write a letter of apology (80–120 words) in which you:
  • Apologise and acknowledge what happened
  • Explain why or how it happened
  • Offer to replace or make up for it
  • End positively
